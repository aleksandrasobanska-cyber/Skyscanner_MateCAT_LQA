Skyscanner German Language LQA Model Instructions

1. Skyscanner’s Way of Writing
Mission Reflection in Writing
 •Modernity: Emphasize simplicity, speed, and straightforwardness in travel planning and booking.
  - Examples:
   * “Planning and booking your trip should be simple, not a chore.”
   * “We're making sure our app and website are super straightforward and speedy.”
   * “Our app and website are available in more than 30 languages.”
 •Sustainability: Highlight sustainable travel and environmental responsibility.
  - Examples:
   * “A 'Greener choice' label which highlights the flights that emit less CO2.”
   * “We buy sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) to help offset any travel we do here at Skyscanner.”
   * “We’re working with the Duke of Sussex, and industry leaders, to form Travalyst.”


2. General Writing & Translation Guidelines
2.1 What Is Good Writing?
 •Always put the traveller first; focus on user benefit and advice.
  - Example:
   * ✅ “Get deals on everything from quick breaks to epic adventures”
   * ❌ “We’re the world’s travel search engine”
 •Avoid excessive adjectives; ensure every sentence is helpful.
 •Always consider the user’s perspective.
2.2 Writing & Translation Guidelines
 •Conversational Style: Translations should sound natural and spoken, not formal or stiff.
 •Clarity and Correctness: Use clear, well-known terms. Translate meaning, not just words.
  - Example:
   * ✅ “Deine Daten. Deine Entscheidung.”
   * ❌ “Deine Daten. Deine Wahl.”
 •Simplicity: Use simple words and short sentences for inclusivity and accessibility.
 •Active Voice: Prefer active over passive, especially in headlines and CTAs.
  - Example:
   * ✅ “Discover our map”
   * ❌ “Discovering our map”
 •Direct Address: Use informal singular “du” (lowercase) for addressing users, unless context requires otherwise (e.g., terms & conditions).
 •Specificity: Provide concrete, useful details rather than vague statements.
 •Personal Touch: Use personal experience to add credibility and detail.
 •Interest: Make content engaging and lively.
 •Honesty: Be transparent about pros and cons.
 •Accuracy: Double-check all factual details (spelling, times, locations, etc.).
 •Exclamation Marks: Use sparingly.

3. Language-specific Translation Guideline
3.1 Grammar and Formatting
 •Follow “Duden. Die deutsche Rechtschreibung” for grammar and spelling. Use “Von Duden empfohlene Schreibung” if multiple options exist.
3.1.1 Punctuation & Spacing
 •Hyphens:
  - Use with numbers + terms (e.g., 80-prozentig, 12-jährig), compound nouns for readability (e.g., Desktop-Publishing).
  - Do not use with established English noun compounds (e.g., Feedback).
  - Examples:
   * DO: 12-jährig, Feedback, Vor- und Nachteile
   * DON’T: 12 jährig, Feed-back, Vor und Nachteile
 •Dashes:
  - Use dash (–) for connecting thoughts or indicating timespans, no spaces.
  - Examples:
   * DO: 2018–2019, Es ist 13:00 Uhr – los geht’s!
   * DON’T: 2018-2019 (hyphen), Es ist 13:00 Uhr - los geht’s! (hyphen), Es ist 13:00 Uhr—los geht’s! (em dash)
 •Ampersands (&):
  - Use only in brand names or UI where space is limited.
  - Always use a non-breaking space before & and –.
 •Quotation Marks:
  - Use German „xyz“ (99-66 rule); single quotes for nested quotes: „xyz ‚xyz‘ xyz.“
  - Examples:
   * DO: „xyz“, Er sagte: „Hallo?“
   * DON’T: "xyz", »xyz«
 •Apostrophe:
  - Use only when necessary, always as ’ (looks like a 9).
  - Examples:
   * DO: Los geht’s
   * DON’T: Los gehts, Los geht`s, Lass’ dich inspirieren
 •Percentage Sign:
  - Use a non-breaking space between number and %.
  - Examples:
   * DO: 50 %
   * DON’T: 50%
 •Ellipsis:
  - Use the correct symbol (…), with a non-breaking space before.
  - Examples:
   * DO: … (correct symbol), Wenn das deine Mutter wüsste …
   * DON’T: ... (three dots), Wenn das deine Mutter wüsste....
3.2 Quantifiers
 •Undefined quantifiers (alle, einzig, manche) and numbers up to twelve are written out as adjectives (e.g., “Hier sind elf Optionen”).
3.3 Addressing Users
 •Use informal singular “du”, “dir”, “deine” (lowercase).
  - DO: Gefallen dir diese Flüge?
  - DON’T: Gefallen Dir diese Flüge? Gefallen Ihnen diese Flüge? Gefallen euch diese Flüge?
 •Exceptions may apply in legal/terms contexts.
3.4 Active Voice
 •Use active voice, avoid nominal style and bureaucratic/formal sentences. Prefer short, direct sentences.
3.5 Formats
3.5.1 Numbers, Dates, and Time
 •Write out numbers up to 12; use numerals for 13+ unless space or clarity requires otherwise.
 •Use full stops for thousands (e.g., 1.534.049).
 •Use commas for decimals (e.g., 299,99 Euro).
 •Always use a non-breaking space between numbers and units, %, etc.
  - DO: 250 €, 50 %, 30 km
  - DON’T: 250€, 50%, 30km
 •No non-breaking space if number is written out (e.g., fünf Kilometer).
 •Dates/times per DIN 5008:
  - DO: am Sonntag, den 14. Mai 2016, 2. Februar 2016, 14:00, 14:00 Uhr
  - DON’T: am Sonntag, 14. Mai 2016, 02. Februar 2016, 02.02.2016, 2.2.2016, 14 Uhr, 14.00 Uhr
  - Use dash with no spaces for ranges: 17.–19. November, Mo.–Fr. 10:00–18:00 Uhr
3.5.2 Currencies
 •Use currency symbols (€/$/£) for prices.
  - Example: “Für nur 50 € in den Urlaub? Kein Problem!”
 •Use full names in editorial/long copy if relevant.
  - Example: “Am Flughafen kannst du dein Geld schnell von Euro in Yen umtauschen.”
3.5.3 Units of Measure
 •Use metric system, with a space between number and unit. Abbreviations are allowed.
  - Examples: 1.500 km, 90 kg, 29 cm
3.5.4 Countries
 •Use official German names and spellings.
3.5.5 URLs
 •No spaces; only hyphens allowed as non-character symbol.
 •All lowercase.
 •No extended/special characters; only Latin characters.
 •For non-Latin languages, keep URLs in English (except EL: use Latin transliteration).
 •Use http://www.url-encode-decode.com/ to check encoding.
 •If URLs are split into multiple strings, reverse order if established translation requires it.
  - Example: city-breaks → escapades-urbanes (CA)
 •If translation is more than two words, split so each part is meaningful.
3.6 Linguistic Sequence
3.6.1 Transliteration
 •Use German alphabet (including ä, ö, ü, ß) in text.
 •For code/URLs, transliterate:
  - ä → ae, ö → oe, ü → ue, ß → ss
 •Follow brand/organization preferred spelling if specified.
3.6.2 Organization Names
 •Use official national organization names; abbreviations allowed after full name is established.
  - Example: “Wir haben mit jemandem beim Bundesministerium für Bildung und Forschung gesprochen. Das BmBF ist der Meinung…”
 •Colloquial shorthands are acceptable if tone is appropriate.
3.6.3 NGO, Company, Association Names
 •Use established German names for international organizations; abbreviations after full name.
  - Example: “Die Weltgesundheitsorganisation (World Health Organization) hat neue Daten bekanntgebeben. Laut der WHO sollen Reisende in Zukunft…”
 •If no German translation or better known by international name, use that (e.g., Greenpeace).
 •For company names, use official spelling per their materials.
 •For product categories, use conversational spelling if not referring to the brand directly.
  - Example:
   * Company-preferred: LEGO Stein, Tempo Tücher
   * Conversational: Legostein, Tempotücher
   * Alternatives: Bausteinchen, Taschentücher
