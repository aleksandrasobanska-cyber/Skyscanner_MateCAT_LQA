Spanish LQA Model Instructions
1. Translation Approach
 • Be creative and transcreate when necessary. Modify wording to ensure natural Spanish, not forced by English structure.
 • Always preserve the original meaning.
 • Avoid literal, word-for-word translations if they sound unnatural in Spanish.
2. Communication Style
 • Use clear, direct, transparent, and open language.
 • Appeal to user emotions.
 • Be accurate and inspiring; make the message stand out.
 • Write as if speaking to a friend; use a conversational tone.
 • Avoid repeating the same words; use synonyms to prevent redundancy.
3. Punctuation and Spacing
 • Use punctuation to clarify meaning and ease reading; avoid unnecessary punctuation.
 • Exclamation Marks: Use sparingly; remove or add as needed for a natural tone.
 • Quotation Marks: Use angular quotation marks (« »), not English-style (“ ”).
 • Hyphens: Rare in Spanish; use only if required by convention.
 • Full Stops: Do not use in buttons, menu text, headers, or titles. Headers should be concise and match English length.
 • Bulleted Lists: Ensure correct punctuation and capitalization.
 • Special Cases: Adjust punctuation and merge sentences to improve readability.
4. Capitalization
 • Titles and Buttons: Do not capitalize every word; follow Spanish capitalization rules.
 • Months and Days: Do not capitalize unless at the start of a sentence.
 • After Colon: Do not capitalize after a colon, except:
  - After greetings in emails/letters.
  - After “Nota:”, “Aviso:”, “Urgente:”.
  - When the text after the colon is a quote.
5. Non-breaking Spaces
 • Use non-breaking spaces (_) for dates and between numbers and symbols.
  - Correct: 18_de_marzo_de_2020, 20_€, Ahorra un 10_% en vuelos
  - Incorrect: 18 de marzo de 2020, 20 €, 10 % de descuento
6. Addressing Users
 • Use informal “tú” for users.
 • Use “nosotros, nos, nuestro” for Skyscanner.
 • Use a conversational tone.
 • Exception: Use formal “usted” only in legal texts.
7. Numbers
 • 0-10: Write out in conversational form, except for prices and percentages.
 • Four-figure numbers: No dot as thousand separator.
 • Numbers over four figures: Use non-breaking space as thousand separator.
 • Decimals: Use a comma as the decimal separator.
8. Anglicisms
 • Avoid English words unless commonly accepted in Spanish, especially for social media and marketing. Decide on a case-by-case basis.
9. Voice
 • Prefer active voice over passive voice.
  - Example: “El hotel ha cancelado tu reserva.” (YES)
  - “Tu reserva ha sido cancelada por el hotel.” (NO)
10. Formats (Dates, Currencies, etc.)
 • Dates: Use DD/MM/YYYY format.
 • In full text, do not capitalize days or months.
  - Correct: El viernes, 2 de septiembre de 2021
  - Incorrect: El Viernes, 2 de Septiembre de 2021
 • Time: Use 24-hour format with colon separator.
  - Correct: Salida hasta las 14:00
  - Incorrect: Salida hasta las 2.00
 • “h” after time is optional; if used, add a non-breaking space before it.
 • For time variables, use “A partir de las %{time}”, not “A partir de la(s) %{time}”.
11. URL Translation Requirements
 • No spaces; use hyphens if needed (e.g., “city-break”, not “city break”).
 • All characters must be lowercase (e.g., “city-break”, not “City-break”).
 • No extended or special characters (e.g., “e”, not “é”).
 • Only Latin characters allowed.
 • For multi-string URLs, reverse order if there is an established Spanish term (e.g., “city-breaks”).
 • Do not translate “Skyscanner” in URLs.
12. Organization Names
 • Research official Spanish names for organizations; use the most common if no official name exists.
 • Do not translate “Skyscanner”.
 • For other companies, NGOs, and associations, use the commonly used Spanish name if available; otherwise, leave in English.
13. Geo-terms
 • Use country names as per RAE or FAO lists.
14. Tax/Legal Terms
 • Ensure accuracy and find the local Spanish equivalent. Research thoroughly; consult Language Lead if unsure.
15. Inclusion
 • Phrase translations to be as gender-neutral as possible.
